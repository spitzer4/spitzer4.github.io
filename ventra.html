Ventra Redesign

Designing Faster, Smarter Transit


A proposal to enhance the Ventra app by transforming it from a reactive search tool into a proactive, data-validated transit assistant, resulting in a 60–70% reduction in time-to-arrival information.


This is a conceptual case study and I am not in any way affiliated with the CTA or Ventra.

My Role

UX Designer, User Researcher

Duration

2 Weeks (Personal Project)

Key Tools

Figma, Google Forms

Focus

Information Architecture & Predictive UX

The Problem & Data Pivot

Chicago's official Ventra app has high adoption but suffers from severe usability friction. User surveys confirmed that the most consistent complaints centered on the confusing, tap-heavy hierarchy for viewing arrival times. Riders are seeking simplicity while moving quickly.

“Opening the app and having to tap multiple times to see your train/bus arrival time.”

The Critical Insight: Despite these flaws, riders still use Ventra daily. This led to a key strategic pivot: improving the flawed, established app (maximizing impact) was a better use of design effort than building a replacement.

Architectural Shift

Mental Models vs. System Logic

The primary challenge was a Mental Model Mismatch. Riders think spatially ("the stop I'm at"), but the app was architected route-first. This forced riders through unnecessary cognitive steps and taps, directly violating the need for glanceable information.

Original Flow (Route-First)

3–4 taps required.

Redesign Flow (Stop-Centric)

1–2 taps required.

Design Solutions: Technical & Predictive

1. Stop-Centric Architecture

I shifted the primary information architecture to be stop-centric, utilizing the rider's current location as the starting point. This implements a system of Progressive Disclosure, showing nearby stops immediately and minimizing taps to the arrival information itself.

  • Principle: Match spatial mental models.
  • Result: **Result:** Eliminates redundant navigation levels.

2. AI Contextualization Layer

A technically sophisticated layer was proposed to predict the most relevant stop or route based on time, location, and user history. This allows the app to function as a proactive assistant, surfacing predictions directly on the home screen.

  • Principle: Proactivity over reactivity.
  • Result: Enables glanceable arrivals with zero taps in many scenarios.

Measured Impact & Data-Backed Framing

60–70%

Efficiency Gain

Reduction in average time-to-arrival information (from 10s to 1–2s).

3 -> 1

Tap Count Reduction

Elimination of up to 2-3 redundant taps in the core user flow.

4 of 6

User Validation

Respondents named "too many taps" as their primary frustration, which the solution directly addresses.

Reflection

This project reinforced the value of data-backed strategy. I initially planned to create a competitive app, but research showed that improving an existing, highly adopted tool maximizes real-world impact. The redesign successfully transitioned the app from a reactive search tool to a proactive assistant, making the experience clearer and smarter.

The core learning was prioritizing architectural change (stop-centric) over simple aesthetic tweaks, demonstrating how deeply integrating technical and statistical insights informs impactful UX design.
